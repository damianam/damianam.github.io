Know your gear series: Harness, fitting to your body and effects to consider. Part 3.

During the first and second part of these articles, we've gone over harness construction and options. This is very important knowledge to order a new harness/container. If you do, and the measurements for it are done properly, you shouldn't worry too much about fitting of the harness. Chances are that the manufacturer will do a good job and your brand new harness will fit you perfectly. However, this third part will help you to verify that. More importantly, if you buy a second hand harness, this article will help you to find out if the harness you are buying is appropriate for you.

Body types and hip rings

The above paragraph holds true for most people. A harness made to measurment will fit perfectly the vast majority of times. But in some cases, you should observe more carefully the harness contruction of your manufacturer of choice, even if you order new. Certain designs might adapt better to your body than others. That depends on the placement of the hip rings and the length of the container.

Torsos and container lenght

Long torsos, when mixed with short containers, present one problem. Think about the point where the lateral go from the container to the MLW. If the hip ring location is right in the lateral junction, the articulation will be too high. The leg straps will hinge in an unnatural point, above the hip, which could make the harness less comfortable than harnesses with the hip ring lower on the MLW. That's why it could be a good idea to get a harness with the hip rings connected below the laterals, if you have a long torso and a short container.

People with short torsos should consider the opposite than people with long torsos, particularly if they have a long container. That can be common for new skydivers that are on the shorter end. In this case, the lateral will be already laying fairly low on the body. That's why having hip rings that connect the leg straps, laterals and MLW, could be a good idea. Otherwise the geometry of the leg straps might not fit properly the body of the wearer.

To accomodate both end of the spectrum, and wearer preferences, some manufacturers might offer both types of articulations. Mirage, for instance, offers both types in their G4s.

Effects of bad harness fitting by sections and positions

A poorly fitting harness could be not just less comfortable, but also compromise your performance and even your safety. The following subsections will dig deeper in this.

Yoke and shoulders

The yoke size depends basically on how wide your chest and shoulders are. A smaller yoke than you need would be uncomfortable and limit the movements in your uppper body. If the MLW around the shoulders is too short and narrow that would also effectively move everything up —handles, 3 rings, chest strap and hip rings—. However, a bigger yoke and shoulder area than you need could create other issues in head down attitudes. A large yoke and long MLW result in a harness too loose at the shoulders. What this means is that when flying head down or steep angles your rig might slide down one shoulder. While this is rarely a safety concern —the yoke would have to be really side and the MLW really long to allow your rig to slide down both shoulders— it is unsettling and uncomfortable while flying. This could distract you and affect your performance.

The sliding becomes more noticeable in certain harnesses with chest rings, since this extra articulation makes the harness more flexible and more prone to this situation. Over-tightening the chest strap can help to compensate a bit this situation, but just to a certain degree. Likewise, if you tend to have your chest strap on the loose side, this could happen more easily.

MLW

The length of the MLW should match the length of your torso. If the MLW is too short, you won't be able to properly tighten your legs straps. That would be uncomfortable, since the front of the leg straps, the part close to the buckle, might be in contact with your inner thigh, without any padding. Besides that, the alignment of the hip rings would be off and the fit would be generally speaking bad and the feeling would be of a rig that limits your movements and is too tight.

However, the opposite could be arguably worse. A MLW that is too long for your torso will allow too much wiggling of your rig. On sit fly the container will move upwards to your head. On head down it will move downwards as we saw above. On belly flying it will wiggle freely and push you around.

Worse than that, your handles will move with your container. That means that instead of being in a fixed position, they will have an area where they might be. When you run out of altitude and you really need to grab your handles, you need to know where they are, as close as possible as the position where they are when you practice your emergency procedures in the ground and on the airplane. There is a natural shift when under canopy, but this displacement should be as small as possible, which is guaranteed with a proper fit.

The shift when under canopy is because your weight rests now in the leg straps, with the MLW connecting that weight to the canopy above your head. As a result, your handles move upwards, but they aren't the only thing moving. The chest strap moves upwards as well. In some extreme cases you can even get hit in the throat or face by it, which is obviously something you should avoid and a very clear indicator of a MLW too long for you. Moreover, if you are a woman and on the busty side, you should also make sure that the chest strap rests on your chest and not below, as other wise the chest strap might hurt you during opening.

Laterals

Laterals are once again one of the parts that are more ignored when testing a second hand harness, but still are a very important part to guarantee a good fit. If they are too short you'll feel like the rig is too tight. But if they are too long your container won't be snug enough.

Laterals too long have a similar effect than a MLW too long, when it comes to keeping the container close to your back. They will allow your rig to lift off your back. When on your belly that means that the rig will move around, and you will have to reach farther for your hand deploy. When sitflying, the wind will catch your rig and fill the space between your back and your rig, making your maneuvers less efficient and your position more difficult to keep under control. Backtracking in a straight line will also be more difficult with your rig moving laterally and pushing you off course.

How do I know if a rig fits me?

A rig made to your measurements should fit snug and tight, but it shouldn't restraint your movements. If you buy a second hand harness, you could check if it fits your properly checking these points. After tightening the straps, your harness should fit like this:

  • You should feel slightly constrained when standing up, feeling the tension of the harness on your shoulders all the way down to your legs. If you don't feel that tension, the MLW might be too long.
  • The cutaway and reserve handles should be immediately below your chest and to the sides. If they are down to your belly is a sign that the MLW is again too long.
  • On the ground you should be able to move your container off your back just a little bit. If you can fit more than one fist between the rig and your lower back (below the lumbar curve) your laterals or MLW might be too long.
  • You shouldn't be able to tighten your chest strap so much that the MLW gets too inward distorted. Rigs with chest rings have more yield in this area.
  • If you can pull one side of your rig below your shoulder while the other side stays in position, the yoke might be too wide for you.
  • When you stand up the junction where the front and back sides of the leg strap join should be slightly below your hip bone, at the point where your leg articulates. Depending on the type of hip ring and leg strap construction your rig has, the ring would sit there, or slightly higher.
  • You should be able to comfortably tighten your leg straps. The padding should cover the webbing at all points. At the same time, the straps shouldn't be loose around your legs when fully tightened.
It is important to note that these points focus on an optimal and comfortable fit. If your rig doesn't fit you as described, it doesn't necessarily mean that it is unsafe for you.

Harness resizing

So you found a nice used container, you paid for it, get it ready to be jumped, and you found out that the harness doesn't fit you. It is uncomfortable, moves around too much, or doesn't really hold you as you think it should. What to do? You can pass it along another jumper whose body is a better fit for that harness, or you can get the harness resized. The price and feasibility of these alterations depend a lot on the parts to be altered and the number of rings on the harness. These are some things to consider:

  • Rigs with rings make resizing and replacing some parts of the harness much easier. Leg straps can be more easily replaced with hip rings than without them. If the rig has chest rings, adjusting the lower MLW is also easier than without them.
  • Shortening the chest strap is the cheapest alteration you can do. Lengthening it is also a fairly cheap and easy procedure. You can even buy an extension that connects to your original chest strap.
  • Shortening the leg straps is a fairly easy task. Making them longer implies replacing or tweaking the whole assembly, including padding. If the leg strap is a single piece, this could be more complicated than in two pieces setups.
  • MLW adjustments are tricky —ie: expensive—, specially without chest rings, or with rigs where the laterals are sewn directly to the MLW.
  • Adjusting the laterals implies adjusting the MLW in some cases, and the back padding and container. It is possible but it could be a fair bit of work.
  • Lastly, modifying the yoke is rarely done, and chances are that you are better off looking for another rig.

Harness sizing per manufacturer

So, someone over internet wants to sell you a rig, and they say that the size is 17+1. Or C17. Or 16.5, 17 +.5. Or something else. Sadly, there isn't a standard way to report harness sizes. Each manufacturer has their own way. However, most manufacturers state the length of the MLW in the data card of their rigs. UPT for instance writes down the length of the MLW followed by the deviation of their standard yoke size (in positive or negative numbers). Sunpath states the size of the yoke with a letter, which goes from smaller to bigger (A, B, C, D...) followed by the length of the MLW. Mirage is more precise, and states the MLW, followed by the length of the leg straps, and the deviation from their standard lateral length. Mirage also has a very handy tool to find out the harness size using your measurements.

Bottom line is that everyone does it differently. You'll have to check case by case. Manufacturers have the measurements of every rig they make, so you can ask them using the serial number of the rig you are interested on.

End of Part 3

As you can see, there is more to harness fitting than “tight” or “loose”. When buying a second hand harness you need to consider all the dimensions of the harness and your body to guarantee a proper fit.
